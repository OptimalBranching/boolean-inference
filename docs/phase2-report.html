boolean-inference · Phase 2 — seeing the propagation work

all outputs below are real program runs cargo run --example phase2_demo

Phase 2 answers one question: given a set of clauses (and optionally an assumption), which variables are thereby forced — and is it even possible at all? Below are concrete instances. For each: what goes in, what the engine prints out, and why that output is the correct answer you can check by hand.

x1=1 fixed true x1=0 fixed false x1=? still free (could be either) ∅ / UNSAT impossible — no assignment satisfies the clauses

Part A — what the clauses alone force

No assumptions. Just feed the clauses and read what must be true.

1A unit clause starts a chain reaction
in · clauses
(x1) ∧ (¬x1 ∨ x2) ∧ (¬x2 ∨ x3)
out
x1=1 x2=1 x3=1 (0 of 3 still free)
why
(x1) forces x1=1. That makes (¬x1 ∨ x2) depend on x2, so x2=1. That in turn forces x3=1 via the third clause. The engine follows the whole cascade — exactly what unit propagation should do.
2Underconstrained — nothing is forced (no over-deduction)
in · clauses
(x1 ∨ x2 ∨ x3)
out
x1=? x2=? x3=? (3 of 3 still free)
why
One clause with three options forces nothing — many assignments satisfy it (e.g. 1,0,0 or 0,1,0). A correct engine must leave all three free here. This is the important negative case: it proves the engine doesn't invent constraints that aren't there.
3An outright contradiction is caught immediately
in · clauses
(x1) ∧ (¬x1)
out
UNSAT — the clauses contradict each other outright
why
x1 is required to be both true and false. No assignment can satisfy both clauses, so the problem is unsatisfiable before any search begins — and the engine reports it at construction time.
4An equivalence plus one fact resolves everything
in · clauses
(¬x1 ∨ x2) ∧ (x1 ∨ ¬x2) ∧ (x1)
out
x1=1 x2=1 (0 of 2 still free)
why
The first two clauses together say x1 = x2. The third pins x1=1. So x2=1 follows. The engine combines a structural constraint with a single fact and lands on the unique answer.

Part B — "what if?" (non-destructive assumptions)

Same clauses, but now we tentatively assume a value for one variable and see the consequence. The base problem is never mutated — each assumption is explored independently. This is precisely what the future search will do at every branch.

5Exclusive-or: each choice forces the other variable
in · clauses
(x1 ∨ x2) ∧ (¬x1 ∨ ¬x2)  — this is x1 XOR x2
base
x1=? x2=?
what-if
assume x1=1 →  x1=1 x2=0
assume x1=0 →  x1=0 x2=1
why
XOR means the two variables must differ. Nothing is forced up front (base is all-free), but the moment you assume one, the other is determined — and in opposite directions for the two assumptions. Both branches are explored from the same untouched base.
6A "failed literal": one assumption self-destructs
in · clauses
(¬x1 ∨ x2) ∧ (¬x1 ∨ x3) ∧ (¬x2 ∨ ¬x3)
base
x1=? x2=? x3=?
what-if
assume x1=1 →  CONTRADICTION — this branch is UNSAT
assume x1=0 →  x1=0 x2=? x3=?
why
Assume x1=1: the first two clauses force x2=1 and x3=1, but the third clause forbids x2 and x3 both being true — dead end. Assume x1=0: the clauses with ¬x1 are satisfied and nothing else is forced. So x1=1 is impossible; a solver would conclude x1 must be 0. The engine detects the doomed branch by propagation alone, no guessing.

What this demonstrates

Across these six instances the engine: ① chains forced consequences, ② refuses to over-deduce when a clause is genuinely ambiguous, ③ catches direct contradictions, ④ fuses structural constraints with facts, ⑤ explores both polarities of an assumption from an untouched base, and ⑥ recognizes a branch that propagation alone proves impossible. That is the complete job of Phase 2 — and the building block the branch-and-reduce search (later phases) repeats at every decision.
